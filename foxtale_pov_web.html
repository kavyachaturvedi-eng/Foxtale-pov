Part 1

The problem

Before we talk about what we are building, let us be precise about what is broken — not at a surface level, but at the level of root cause. Everything in this document follows from this section.

1.1What Foxtale's supply chain looks like today

Foxtale sells skincare products across 12 channels and sources everything from third-party manufacturers. On paper, the demand planning process is well-designed. In practice, it runs entirely on informal tools with no enforcement layer.

Sales channels12 total: Amazon, Flipkart, Nykaa, Myntra, Meesho, Purplle, Tira (marketplaces) + Blinkit, Zepto, Swiggy (quick commerce) + Website (D2C) + Offline/GT
ManufacturersThree third-party contract manufacturers supply all products. Lead time from PO to warehouse receipt: 3 to 4 weeks per manufacturer.
ERP / WMSUnicommerce. Inventory source of truth. Closing stock syncs daily. In an ideal world, all procurement flows through and back to Unicommerce.
Planning tool todayMicrosoft Excel. Version-named files (V17, V18, V19…) shared over WhatsApp and email. No version control. No access control. No audit trail. No validation logic.
Demand submissionGrowth team fills the Excel file per channel and shares it with the supply chain team each month. SCM team then manually computes all procurement quantities.

Failure 1 — No accountability before forecast changes land

When the growth team revises a demand figure — even a 25% reduction on a high-volume SKU — there is no system check, no justification requirement, and no approval gate. The change is accepted at the moment of submission. By the time anyone notices, the manufacturer has already begun production or ordered raw materials based on the previous number.

The real cost
This is not a process problem. It is a working capital problem. Every unjustified demand reduction after a manufacturer commitment represents either wasted procurement spend or a renegotiation penalty.
Failure 2 — 10% of orders are emergency M1 purchase orders

A Month 1 PO means an order raised in the same month the goods are needed. In a business shipping approximately 30 lakh units per month, 10% as M1 means 3 lakh units per month procured in emergency mode. Both Sunscreen Matte (43,167 units) and Sunscreen Dewy (54,687 units) are M1 POs in the February 2026 cycle — the two highest-volume SKUs — going to the same manufacturer simultaneously.

Failure 3 — Simultaneous stockouts and dead stock in the same cycle

Ceramide Moisturizer is 18 days from running out. Sunscreen Matte is sitting at 67 days of stock — 22 days above the target. Both conditions exist at the same time, in the same company, in the same month. This is not bad luck. It is the direct output of a forecasting process with no feedback mechanism and no system-enforced accuracy standard.

Failure 4 — Silent formula corruption in every PO calculation

The PO calculation depends on four inputs: closing inventory, in-transit stock, GRN pending, and open POs. Closing inventory is the only one that auto-syncs from Unicommerce. The other three are manually entered by the supply chain team into Excel every cycle. Any transcription error propagates silently through the entire M1, M2, and M3 calculation. The formula produces a wrong answer that looks exactly like a right answer.

Highest technical risk
Every inventory forecasting platform operating at this scale auto-syncs these three data points from the WMS. Running them manually is not a process choice — it is a structural accuracy risk that makes the formula output unreliable regardless of how well the demand forecast is constructed.
Failure 5 — Fear buffer contagion after every stockout

When one SKU runs out, the team's response is to add safety buffer to every SKU in the catalogue — not just the one that stocked out. After each stockout event, company-wide Days on Hand increases by approximately 7 to 10 days. Working capital is locked unnecessarily across products that had no supply risk.

Failure 6 — Every decision's reasoning is permanently lost

When a forecast is revised, when a buffer is added, when a demand target changes — the reasoning behind that decision is never recorded. Six months later, no one can answer why a particular choice was made. V18, V19, V20 are the entire audit trail. The institutional knowledge of the business lives in people's memories and chat histories, not in a system that can be reviewed, improved, or held accountable.

"The problem is not that the team is making bad decisions. The problem is that the system makes it impossible to make consistently good ones."

The situation

The business mechanics that make this problem expensive — and the constraints that any solution must respect. A replacement system must replicate this logic exactly.

2.1How demand planning actually works
Secondary demandWhat end customers buy from the marketplace or website. The growth team's monthly target. Measured in units sold by the channel.
Primary demandWhat Foxtale must procure from manufacturers and hold in its warehouse. Always calculated from secondary — never set independently. Always larger than secondary for marketplace channels.
The 45-day ruleFoxtale's warehouse should always hold 45 days of upcoming secondary demand in stock. Manufacturing + shipping = ~30 days. Safety buffer = 15 days. Total = 45 days target DOH.
When Primary = SecondaryWebsite (D2C) and Offline/GT only. Foxtale ships directly — no fulfilment centre buffer layer. No speculative stocking for GT to avoid RTV risk.

3.2Feature 1 — Intelligent demand input with model-generated baseline
01
Foundation · ML Layer
The growth team no longer opens a blank form. They open a pre-filled table.

For every SKU and every channel, the system has already produced a model-generated baseline forecast for the next four months. The team reviews, adjusts what they know needs adjusting, and submits. Every override becomes training data for the next cycle.

Three model tracks: Stable SKUs (AZ/BZ — ~85% auto-forecastable by statistical model). Volatile SKUs (AX/BX/AY — ~65% model + human review, range forecast). New launches (CX — ~10%, analogue lookup, human leads). Confidence shown per cell: green (high, AZ/BZ), yellow (review, AX/BX), red (new launch, CX).

Human-in-loop flow: Accept (auto-submits) · Override + justify + tag (reason stored as training data) · Add context (Sale event, Campaign drop, MRP change — captured systematically for first time).

Feedback loop: Each cycle close triggers MAPE calculation per channel team, bias detection, override pattern analysis, and monthly model retrain. The model improves every cycle.

→ Time spent per cycle drops from 2–3 days to 3–4 hours. Blank-slate guesswork becomes data-anchored review.
3.3Feature 2 — Pre-submission validation gates (Gate A + Gate B)
02
Accountability
Before any submission is accepted, two automatic checks run. These are not suggestions. They are gates.

Gate A — Demand variance alert: System compares current entry to M-1, M-2, M-3 prior submissions. If |Variance %| exceeds the threshold, the Submit button is disabled. A justification drawer appears — free text (min 20 characters) + reason tag (Competitor launch / Sale event / Campaign change / Product issue / MRP change / Other). Both required before submission re-enables.

VARIANCE CHECK
Variance % = (Current - Prior Submission) / Prior Submission × 100
AX SKUs: threshold = 10% · All others: threshold = 20% · Configurable by Analytics

Gate B — AOP alignment check: Sum of demand GMV (units × MRP) across all channels compared against Finance AOP monthly target. Deviation above threshold triggers an Analytics notification. Warning gate — does not block submission.

Submit button disabled on flagJustification text + reason tag Manufacturer impact previewAOP deviation warning Per-tier thresholdsConfigurable without engineering
→ The first gate in the history of Foxtale's demand process that fires before the manufacturer is impacted, not after.

Part 4

How it works

A single demand cycle from the moment the growth team opens the platform to the moment a purchase order lands at the manufacturer. This is the full user journey after Phase 1 is live.

4.1The demand cycle — step by step
01
Growth team opens the platform on the first working day of the demand window
The platform shows each channel's demand input screen with baseline figures pre-populated from the previous 3 months of actuals. For each SKU across M1 to M4, the team reviews the baseline and adjusts for known factors.
02
Pre-submission checks run in real time as they type
If a change exceeds the SKU's variance threshold, the Submit button is disabled and a red justification drawer appears. The submitter cannot proceed without a written reason and a reason tag.
03
AOP alignment check fires on the full submission
Total demand GMV for the month compared to the Annual Operating Plan. If material deviation, Analytics team is notified. Warning, not a block.
04
Submission locked and versioned
System immediately computes primary demand for every SKU and channel, pulling inventory data from Unicommerce in real time. Any revision creates a new version and re-runs all checks.
05
M1, M2, M3 PO quantities calculated per SKU per manufacturer
M1 POs flagged in red. Any PO that deviates significantly from the prior cycle flagged for Analytics review. Output grouped by manufacturer.
06
SCM team receives the PO quantities for review
Interface shows old quantity, new quantity, variance from prior cycle, and growth team's justification if one was required. Approver sees all context needed to decide in 30 seconds.
07
SCM approves with one click
Logged: who, when, with what comment. Analytics team can see this log at any time. The approver cannot be the submitter.
08
Approved POs auto-raised on Unicommerce
Manufacturer notified. No manual re-entry. No email required. The SCM team does not touch a spreadsheet.

Where it can fail

Every supply chain planning platform fails in predictable ways. Having reviewed 15+ live platforms at similar scale, these are the failure modes designed against — not to discover after go-live.

5.1Risk register
Failure pointProbImpactHow we prevent it

5.2Open queries — must confirm before coding begins

These are not design decisions. They are factual questions about business rules that only Foxtale can answer. No assumptions will be made.

Q1
Demand cycle dates
What is the fixed open and close date each month? Can demand be revised after submission within the same cycle, and if so, how many revisions are allowed?
Q2
Variance threshold
Is the alert threshold a single global figure (20%), or does it vary by ABC/XYZ tier? Is the AX threshold already decided as 10%?
Q3
Approval chain
Who is notified first — growth team or analytics? Who has authority to approve a demand change? What is the expected SLA in hours? Is Slack the agreed approval surface?
Q4
45-day rule flexibility
Is the 45-day buffer fixed for all SKUs, or does it vary — e.g., sunscreen higher in March to May, quick commerce channels lower due to faster replenishment cycles?
Q5
B2B outright channels
Are Nykaa B2B, Purplle, and Tira treated identically to B2C marketplace channels for primary demand derivation, or does the outright model change the formula?
Q6
SNOP date
Is the S&OP review meeting on a fixed day each month? Who sets it? This is a critical variable in the M1 formula — determines consumption from SNOP to PO raise date.
Q7
Manufacturer lead times
Is lead time currently tracked per manufacturer or not at all? A 60-day lead time effectively makes every M3 PO an M1. This determines which PO bucket each demand signal falls into.
Q8
Offline/GT submission
Is Offline/GT demand submitted by the same growth team or by a separate sales team on a different cadence?
Q9
Marketing spend granularity
Is daily or weekly spend data available from Meta Ads Manager, Google Ads, Amazon Ads Console, or Blinkit/Zepto dashboards? Monthly totals exist in Excel but daily/weekly granularity is needed for the model to learn demand-lag from spend spikes.
Q10
Sale event calendar
Does a record exist of which months had major platform sale events (Amazon GIF, Flipkart BBD, Nykaa Pink Friday, Republic Day) and the approximate observed demand lift per channel? Without this, the model treats sale-event months as unexplained outliers and will systematically under-forecast every sale month.
